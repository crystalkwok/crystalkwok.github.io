Scale Consolidation by Making Building Trucks Easier

CaseStack combats frequent struggles in logistics using consolidation, a well-known strategy giving CaseStack its highest margins in recent years.

Consolidation groups small orders from different customers into a huge truck (load) to ship to a retailer. The ensured route from only Point A to Point B lowers cost and resources by giving it a higher chance of arriving on time. When you ship small orders individually, it often stops at multiple points, increasing the chances of shipping going wrong.

Different colors = different customers' orders

While a small team has successfully organized their current number of orders into bigger trucks, their manual process with keyboard shortcuts and Excel sheets is cumbersome, not efficient, and more prone to error with additional customers and different types of orders. Currently, this team spends over 15 hours a week just building different trucks, let alone the extra time spent fixing problems created by their manual process.

Currently done by manipulating & uploading this sheet

How can we help CaseStack’s consolidation team manage more consolidation in a more efficient (saving > 15 hours every week), automated way, with less problems as the program expands?

Our product and engineering team decided to create an algorithm that would take away a lot of the manual work (aka the Bin Packing Problem). My job was the learn what industry knowledge needed to be included in the algorithm and how to include it easily, and how the consolidation team needed to work with the algorithm output via an experience in order for our business to adopt the new algorithm.

Over 15 Hrs Saved with 100% Team Adoption

Since an algorithm to replace their manual Excel manipulation was guaranteed to save over 15 hours, our product success metric was if the team was willing to adopt the new tool. Their hesitancy was two things; first, that an algorithm could not account for the needed flexibility they, our customers, and retailers needed to make necessary preferences work. With Excel, they could make any and all changes needed. However, the cost of the flexibility was data inaccuracy, because moving data around often caused numbers that shouldn't be touched to be changed, and for data to be lost in the transfer.

Second, they often had fears and asked would this replace my job? We acknowledged that fear, but we assured them that their value was not in manipulating Excel cells, but rather their logistics knowledge essential for the high margin program to work in reality with real trucks & products.

To mitigate and encourage adoption, we worked closely with the team to understand what they needed, what knowledge they wanted to apply themselves, and how we could prevent data inaccuracies.

Research & Design Process

We conducted contextual observations and usability testings to grasp their context, see the process in real-time, see real data, and learned about the little hacks and pain points that wouldn’t have been mentioned (emails and post-its).

With each iterated design idea and exploration, we gathered user feedback and discussed the pros and cons to decide which would better fit the user and their context.

We’ll first start off with how the team can change the end algorithm result, then work backwards to the other settings and data accuracies the consolidation team needed to adopt the product and find value.

Analyzing & Changing Truck Building is Cumbersome & Corrupts Data

Intuitive interfaces and interactions to understand & change the algorithm result lets the team apply tribal knowledge to save money & time.

Some trucks can fit more volume than the data calculates because you could fit some products in tiny spaces. The team risks it and overestimates trucks from their knowledge of customers’ patterns to potentially save money. Tribal knowledge, or learned experience, creates a need for flexibility past what our algorithm can predict through the data. Even though they built trucks themselves previously, they still always reviewed what they had built to improve it. However, previous attempts in Excel to apply “tribal knowledge” cause data corruption, and is time-consuming to figure out and move data around in different trucks. You can imagine their horror when they lost many orders’ data in their clipboard and had to find it again, or when the data columns didn’t match in sheet being copied to.

Design Goal: Create an easy way for them to review & manage orders in trucks to apply their knowledge to improve accuracy to save time & money

We researched to only include information needed to make decisions and compare with each other, and avoid information overload and useless functionality, since their excel often included everything. I showed them multiple designs with different elements (grid, cards, etc.) to understand their process & thoughts. Their flow is below.

1

Check the Algorithm's Work & Data Used via the Totals & Variance Check

First, overviews need tables for quick scanning to figure out if the # of trucks or data looks off. The first step was to look at the total weight, volume, and more for each destination and see if the number of trucks produced from the algorithm seemed at least logical. We highlight the total numbers and variance between different data sets used across each load and destination, revealing potential data discrepancies that may cause too many or too few trucks.

** For reflection, I would probably make the totals at the top, so you could read the Destination Name along with the totals before digging in more

2

Compare Truck Details to Analyze Any Opportunity to Reduce # of Trucks

The consolidation team wanted to then compare loads’ information first to balance the trucks or possibly reduce trucks. If one truck seemed extremely light, maybe it can be cancelled after moving some orders to other trucks. The list view in the overview makes focusing on each individual truck’s information easily scannable to compare the totals across weight, volume, and more between the different trucks going to the same Destination. After comparing, they’ll decide which truck might need some orders to switch to another truck.

3

Analyze & Move Orders inside the Truck to Improve the Result

After deciding what truck to look into, they’ll need to see the details of the individual orders to know what they can move to other trucks.

While they have already decided on the first truck to look into for the individual orders, we needed a way to still show all trucks’ information in the same Origin/Destination, since they expect to click between trucks frequently when deciding what to move, and still be comparing information to decide the resulting truck.

The design is different in this step, even though it’s the same information previously with the table design. Our users noted that it was imperative that truck information at the last step was easily scannable because that’s what they focused on. However, the main focus at this step was the orders. Cards for truck information at the top at this step make it easy to switch and click between to quickly get a sense of the details inside, don’t take up as much valuable space vertically, make it easy to compare the different truck’s information at a glance, and provide a good visual differentiation between the trucks and orders below, as opposed to its table view in Review Consolidation. If any changes are needed, their eyes move down to identify what orders to move.

The orders information was now the main focus and had to be easily scannable to quickly decide between the orders, using the table design again to compare between weight, volume, and other information.

4

Smallest or Largest Orders are Often Moved First

Orders with highest and lowest cube, highest and lowest weight need to be easily findable and sorted for.

An intuitive interface and interaction to manage the algorithm result helps easily analyze and change truckloads with extensive logistics knowledge past the algorithm. This helps save the company money, and avoid time wasted on last-minute scrambling and fixing data corruption.

Rework & Time Wasted from Data Inaccuracy

More checks, accurate information, and correct settings immediately save time by reducing double-checking, checking multiple places for information, and correcting mistakes in the future.

Some data from the retailers may not always be accurate, such as product weight or volume, which could result in too few or too many trucks planned. They haven’t been able to get some of the more accurate data, such as product weight in our system, because it was separated in Excel.

Some customers pulled out of shipping one destination, but not the others. The trucking team had a preference for carriers on particular Origin/Destination paths. They rely on separate Excel sheets or their emails to know what preferences to apply. Using Excel to apply preferences and get more accuracy, ironically, caused a lot of data inaccuracies, such as data getting lost or accidentally changed in the process of transferring to other sheets. Inaccuracies from the flexible preferences & bad data cause stress late in the process, as the team pushes to correct errors via email to communicate with multiple parties.

Design Goal: Increase data accuracy, apply & maintain preferences & flexibility easily, and bring visibility to fix errors immediately

Get accurate right away-- know errors from the retailer right away without spending time down the line fixing it. Know discrepancies in product data in our system vs the retailer's data to know which one is more accurate to use for truck building.

Matching with our system means more accurate data for truck building. For example, viewing specific product information in a customer’s order is used for comparing our data with the retailer’s product data to identify discrepancies and use the most accurate product data for weight and volume to build more accurate trucks.

We provide clarity and visibility to any mistakes found immediately, such as the customer not existing or settings incorrect in our system, after the initial data upload to avoid spending time fixing mistakes later on.

Different flexible settings to make the algorithm work and for adoption

We researched patterns on how customers, carrier preferences, and other data commonly changed so we could keep flexibility for customization, but also easy maintainability. We apply those preferences into the algorithm, so the team won’t hunt for and manually apply them, and cause more data corruption. To avoid data corruption, we don’t allow for edits in the fields that shouldn’t be changed. Using more accurate data and bringing in preferences automatically means shipping correctly, so it won’t cause issues down the line for everyone involved. Time is saved by reducing the need to look in multiple places, double-check, or correct later on.

Small Usability Improvements Found in Beta

There are some things you can’t predict that would be valuable to users, even during usability testing. The only way to really know is to let the users play with it, in their own context, with real data. Here are selected 2 results:

  1. In the error sheet that shows data inaccuracies and issues, their first instinct is to use the Excel filter to see all the issues for each error. We need consistent error messages to create a concise list in Excel.
  2. The “X” on our full page modals weren’t quite as noticeable, and they often reached for the browser’s back button. We made a simple change to add a background to the “X” to make it accentuated, and they started using that button instead of the browser’s back button.

100% Team Adoption & Saving > 15 Hrs per Week

From our testing, our current solution has saved 3 hours per week for 5 people each. That’s 15 hours every week! That also doesn’t include the amount of time saved not manually building trucks as they expand to other retailers, such as Target. This also saves more time through less emails and corrections due to increased data accuracy.

We assured the team throughout the process that the company valued their logistics knowledge, not their keyboard shortcuts on Excel. The team, although not initially open, saw the value of good, clear, easy-to-use design, only achievable by consistent research with them for their expertise. We did not simply create a purely engineering-led product by releasing an algorithm and claiming it was better. Because it was user-led, fulfilled their needs, and solved pain points, and we constantly looked to them for feedback, they fully adopted this product to use every week to save them money and time in order to expand the business. Mentioned in Tracking Loads, success in consolidation’s ability to ship on time than when shipping their individual orders encourages other retailers to want to refer us to their other supplies (ex: Popchips or Neutrogena) based on our successes, therefore increasing revenue.
