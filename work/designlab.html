DESIGN LAB

Human Computer Interaction research on improving peer feedback in online education

This experiment was conducted during my time as a Human-Computer Interaction research assistant for the Design Lab. This experiment was headed by Catherine Hicks and Vineet Pandey, with the lab headed by Don Norman, Jim Hollan, and Scott Klemmer. This experiment explored how to improve peer feedback in online classes, collaborating with PeerStudio at Stanford. This experiment was mostly headed by Cat and Vineet, while Rachel and I created artifacts, helped with experimental design, data code (qualitative), read and found papers, helped with creating interfaces for their experiments, and presented at the UCSD Computer Science & Engineering 2015 Undergraduate Poster Session.

challenge

Peer Feedback is Unreliable but Necessary

So far, MOOCs (massive online open classes) have shown to be a solution to make education more accessible. [1] [2] [3] While there are still many issues with MOOCs, including a low retention rate, another problem is the lack of quality in the peer feedback provided in those classes. Peer feedback is needed of MOOCs, because there are too many students to a single teacher. However, peer reviews without much instruction lead to grader inaccuracy, inconsistency, and even the perception is that peer feedback in general is unreliable. The quality of feedback is low compared to a teacher’s or expert's reviews [1] . With peer feedback, the quality of education in MOOCs drops [1].

Like most psychology experiments, the problem space was initially explored by researching other experiments and papers.

Previous Research

Communication and expectations lost online

Communication, norms, and expectations are often learned through cues through language, assessments, and social presence in classrooms.

Proposed Method

Context and training provide more support

Experiments on the “framing effect” (changing behavior based on how information is presented and adjusting the environment) has hinted applications for peer reviews. Possibly communicating and framing the experience on how an expert would approach the review, and providing more context to the situation would train novices to think more like experts, also known as "context scaffolding".

Hoped Outcome

Higher quality peer reviews and feedback

The hypothesis is that context scaffolding can train novices to pay more attention to become more like how experts would see give feedback-- giving fundamental principles integral to success. This hopes to reduce biases, rather than focus on the nonessential parts, therefore increasing the quality of the review by making it more reliable and trustworthy.

measuring success

Measuring Quality

Other studies show that the difference between experts and novices are the types of feedback they give, and experts usually focused on deep feedback as opposed to surface feedback.

deep features

parts of the assignment that rest on the fundamental principles that are integral to the work's success

"You could focus on the successes of your previous experience to show the value you brought at your previous job."

surface features

cosmetic, non-essential, and individual choices, such as grammar and word choices

"Make sure to use a more legible font."

method

Experimental vs. Control

With the experimental group, we gave them training on how an expert would review a resume and provided more context on the job description, helping to frame the participant to think like an expert.

With the control group, we simply gave them basic training on the experiment. At the end, we asked both to give feedback on a resume.

outcome

experimental group

  • gave more diverse feedback (deep and surface)
  • gave more positive feedback

control group

  • focused on surface features

improvements

More Questions -- What Else?

I think this experiment was greatly thought out and methodical. However, since peer feedback and figuring out the quality is so qualitative, I also have more questions. Of course training would make an impact, but what else could it be? Was it really the training, or just the sheer amount of information we passed along? Or even how long the reviewer was in the experiment-- if they were in the experiment longer, it could make the user believe that they should try harder on their peer feedback, increasing the quality. What if we gave bad training? Would the feedback still improve? If it does, clearly it wasn't the idea of training them that improved peer feedback-- so what could it be? Simply being forced to put more time into it? I would also separate out the job context with the training into 2 separate experiments, to see what really impacted what. This topic definitely needs more research, which has also been started by many others, like this one.

takeaways

Be Methodical; Define Success

This was the first psychology experiment I was really involved in, where I helped do some of the data coding due to their time constraint. Rachel and I are so grateful to be involved in the process. It took a lot of hard work from Cat and Vineet before they could even start on the experiment-- researching and reading ahead of time before they could even start tackling the problem. You must be prepared and methodical about your research ahead of time. Being a researcher is hard work, and your work is constantly being questioned.

Even as I demonstrated in the improvements section, people will find reasons to poke holes in your research, unsure of if the research is truly valid. Your experiment is often tweaked to see if the impact is still there, to gain a better understanding, or to present another reason on why the impact occurred. Researchers have to be so careful of making claims of causation, when it could have been correlation. Putting yourself out there with a published paper is risky, but that's why it takes so much hard work during the actual experiment. I also learned to remember to define and find measurements of success-- this is what truly marks if the impact you desired was made. This is important to the claim you are making with your experiment.
