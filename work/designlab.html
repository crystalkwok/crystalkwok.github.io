DESIGN LAB

Human Computer Interaction research on online education and peer feedback

I previously conducted Human-Computer Interaction research at the Design Lab with Dr. Catherine Hicks and Vineet Pandey, with the lab headed by Don Norman, Jim Hollan, and Scott Klemmer. We explored how to improve peer feedback, collaborating with PeerStudio at Stanford. I created artifacts, help with experimental design, data code (qualitative), read and find papers, and help with creating interfaces for their experiments.

Our future goals include doing more experimental design with Oppia (Google affiliates).

DURATION: April 2015 - Present

ROLE: Research Assistant, UX / UI Designer

TOOLS: Illustrator, InDesign, Experimental Design, InvisionApp

COLLABORATORS: Catherine Hicks, Vineet Pandey, Rachel Chen

DELIVERABLES:  Final Presentation   | IDP

On a side note, this experience was extremely valuable to me, to see how psychological research can be applied in the HCI realm, and also being around so many intelligent and amazing people. Listening to people consider different ideas, share their thoughts, and share research on current topics is always great, especially when hearing about the complexities and different dimensions in all different topics in HCI. I also learned while at the Design Lab to take control and intiative of my own growth and learning and goals, where I can ask for a change in direction if I don't see what I am doing currently fitting my future overall goal, or if I wish to do something different. The brilliant minds there have also taught me to continually be curious, and to continue to ask questions (and the right ones!) And ultimately, never being afraid to fail and to get feedback on your work, because although your ego may hurt a bit at first, it will ultimately be beneficial to hear from different people about your work.

Poster on Peer Feedback

presented at the UCSD CSE undergraduate research poster session

Context scaffolding includes training users think more like experts on certain areas (ex:resumes), and giving more context on the situation (ex: including a job description).

HYPOTHESIS: with context scaffolding, users will pay more attention to the "deep" features which rest on the fundamental principles which are integral to the workâ€™s success, such as the argument and logic of an essay, while untrained users will pay more attention to "surface" features", which are typically cosmetic, non-essential, and individual, such as the grammar and word choices of an essay.

WHY: Context scaffolding reduces bias by guiding novices through different review stages, so novices are able to critique in the same manner that experts do, effectively solving the problem with peer feedback quality.
