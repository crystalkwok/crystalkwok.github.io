DESIGN LAB

Human Computer Interaction research on peer feedback in online education

I previously conducted Human-Computer Interaction research at the Design Lab with Dr. Catherine Hicks and Vineet Pandey, with the lab headed by Don Norman, Jim Hollan, and Scott Klemmer. We explored how to improve peer feedback, collaborating with PeerStudio at Stanford. I created artifacts, help with experimental design, data code (qualitative), read and find papers, and help with creating interfaces for their experiments.

Our future goals include doing more experimental design with Oppia (Google affiliates).

DURATION: April 2015 - Present

ROLE: Research Assistant, UX / UI Designer

TOOLS: Illustrator, InDesign, Experimental Design, InvisionApp

COLLABORATORS: Catherine Hicks, Vineet Pandey, Rachel Chen

Poster on Peer Feedback

presented at the UCSD CSE undergraduate research poster session

the challenge

In massive online open classes (MOOCs), there are often too many students to a single teacher, making grading and feedback from teachers impossible. This makes peer feedback the next solution. However, peer feedback is often seen as unreliable and the quality of feedback is often low from peers as opposed to teacher's.

our approach and process

Context scaffolding includes training users think more like experts on certain areas (ex:resumes), and giving more context on the situation (ex: including a job description), and we believe context scaffolding is a potential solution to the peer feedback problem with MOOCs.

Our hypothesis is that with context scaffolding, users will pay more attention to the "deep" features which rest on the fundamental principles which are integral to the workâ€™s success, such as the argument and logic of an essay, while untrained users will pay more attention to "surface" features", which are typically cosmetic, non-essential, and individual, such as the grammar and word choices of an essay.

This is because context scaffolding reduces bias by guiding novices through different review stages, so novices are able to critique in the same manner that experts do, effectively solving the problem with peer feedback quality.

the outcome

PeerStudio Interface Design

the challenge

Rachel and I were tasked with designing interfaces to run the peer feedback experiment. We wanted to make sure the experience was seamless, and that users were able to reach their goals of getting their resumes critiqued and learning about critiquing. In addition, we had to put in the constraints experimenters wanted to ensure the quality of feedback and their experiment.

our approach and process

We first created paper wireframes separately, and then came together to discuss the pros and cons of each, and what direction we wanted to head in. I also created a user flow to show the process a user would go through during the experiment. Together, Rachel and I created hi-fidelity prototypes and put them into InvisionApp, a prototyping tool.

the outcome

Rachel and I created a hi-fidelity prototype in Illustrator, then we put it into InvisionApp to show the interaction and flow a user would go through.
