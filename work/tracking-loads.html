Tracking Loads

Scaling more loads to reach retailers on time (goal: 80% to 96% on time) to reduce late fees and increase trust for referrals

Make Scalabale, Increase Reliability, Reduce Late Fees

To ship our customer’s orders, CaseStack coordinates with carriers and warehouses to ensure truckloads (shortened to loads) make it to the retailers, such as Walmart. However, carriers are finicky — they’ll say they’re coming, yet push back their estimated time of arrival (ETA). Set appointment times are missed from miscommunication, or they’ll drop.

??!?!?! Stuff happens before the carrier gets to the warehouse??!

Currently, Casestack employs Google Sheets to communicate with multiple parties if/when a carrier will arrive at the warehouse. However, because of the information clutter in the current system, keeping up with carrier updates while shipping to more retailers has been difficult. Therefore, CaseStack hasn’t recovered as fast to ship more loads on time even with carrier changes. Less on-time loads mean fines for us, and not delivering on our promise of reliability.

How can we deliver more loads on time to ensure reliability and no fines by tracking loads better to combat finicky carriers & drivers?

This is the dreaded Google Sheet

Increase On Time Percentage 80% to 96%, Less Loads Pushed

Casestack wanted to scale and increase this business because shipping full truckloads to retailers has given this company its highest margins. However, meeting retailer demands for shipping on time was becoming increasingly important as we shipped more loads, as this was one of the metrics retailers used to determine if they wanted to continue doing business with this supplier (ex: Popchips or Dove).

Experiences with the Google Sheet showed that keeping track of so many loads was unscalable, and they needed a new solution. The business success metric was to increase on-time percentage from 80% to 96%. Not only does this help avoid late fees, it could improve relationships with retailers, who would refer our services to suppliers struggling to ship on time. This would therefore increase our revenue.

Due to the lack of data we could accurately gather for increasing efficiency and time saved on the Google Sheets, we decided a simple product success metric would be less loads pushed out to the next day. The goal was to make sure all loads to shipping today left by today so that it would more likely be on time. If they knew about potential delays faster, parties could react more quickly to find new carriers and change schedules to ensure shipping on the same day, therefore making it to the destination on time.

Focus on Users Impacted by Scalability

Although we discovered 3 user groups based on our research, we decided to focus on two, since the first main goal was improving the timeliness and accuracy of information provided by those 2 parties, important for making immediate decisions to ensure loads arrive on time.

CaseStack — people who work with carriers to get updated ETAs, and get carriers to reach the warehouses on time.

Warehouse — people who work at the warehouse, who “check in” carriers once they arrive, handle updates at the warehouse, and communicate when carriers have left with the product.

Casestack user managing trucks, Warehouse user checking in a driver

We conducted qualitative research with over 10 participant interviews remotely and in person at warehouses and offices. We conducted contextual inquires as we observed Warehouse users check-in drivers, and CaseStack users remotely deal with issues as they came about.

Load Status Key to Determining Priority & Next Steps

As we observed CaseStack users, they speedily and repeatedly scanned through the lists of loads on multiple sheets, muttering where they presumed the load was — was it coming soon? Or already past 2 hours at the warehouse?

They often compared the current time to the time written in 3 different areas. If a time was filled out in these 3 areas, it indicated where the truckload was. If the current time was nearing or passed one of these times, that indicated taking action to ensure accuracy or hinted at issues to follow up on.

Knowing where the truck was (load status) indicated their goals and what action to take next. The load statuses were:

Not Arrived-- the carrier gives an estimate when they would “check in” to the warehouse via an ETA. The goal is to get the assigned truck to the warehouse.

Arrived-- the carrier indicates what time they actually arrived at the warehouse via a Check In Time. The goal is to get the truck loaded and leaving the warehouse within a particular time frame to reach the destination on time.

Checked Out-- the carrier indicates what time they actually left the warehouse via a Check Out Time. Let out a sigh of relief because this is considered done (for now)!

For example, the current time passing the ETA without a Check In time prompts CaseStack users to follow up with the carrier on reasons for being late, and for a more accurate ETA. But what’s the point of tracking loads, and what’s considered priority?

They addressed priority items to prevent any loads from not being able to ship on that day, and therefore be considered late, such as no truck available, or product not loaded onto the truck to leave on time.

Lack of Understanding, Focus, and Awareness for Priority Items

Notifications, simplified colors, and a Status Indicator increase clarity, reduce cognitive load, direct attention to resolve priority issues.

As we learned, load status was key to understanding what was priority. However, copious colors were added subjectively by individual users to highlight importance, making understanding where each load was difficult. While colors do indicate different things, the abundant amount resulted in a lack of focus. On top of that, they wasted time scanning and looking at every single line on multiple pages because they had to do a time comparison. If the current time had passed any of the key 3 times on any load, it would become higher priority. Due to the colors, lack of standardization, and the time-wasting action of scanning every load for a time comparison, they couldn’t easily tell which ones took priority to start resolving issues for.

Trying to solve the visual clutter with colors — how do we highlight the important things without it being overwhelming?

Design Goal: Direct attention to priority & understand the situation immediately to determine resolutions

We researched with users, explored, and iterated on how to strategically bring attention without visual overload.

A brief look at the thought processes behind the initial ideation (left), CaseStack Status Iterations (middle) and Warehouse Status Iterations (right)

Simplified colors and the visual time bar reduce the cognitive effort to quickly understand severity and make timely decisions. Intentionally chosen colors bring attention to loads that need action. Time bar visualizations help quickly visualize and compare which might take priority. We also use “20m time elapsed” and “20m time past” to reduce cognitive effort and show the severity more accurately, since they are currently doing the math in their heads.

Notifications direct attention to the priority issues real-time so they can quickly resolve them without searching. For example, key moments such as 30 minutes after a carrier’s ETA has passed without checking in or 2 hours after arriving at the warehouse without checking out indicates potential issues to resolve next for the Warehouse and Casestack users without scanning multiple pages.

CaseStack View (left) and Warehouse View (right)

Information Overload, Wrong Context, Unfocused Goals

Simplified, contextualized, focused, individual views for different users for quicker understanding. Balancing text & visuals for clarity yet less clutter.

After conducting research with Casestack users, I showed my designs to Warehouse users in excitement after receiving great feedback. However, Warehouse users were quite confused on why certain things were emphasized when they didn’t need it. They said they cared mostly after the driver checked in, while Casestack users were more concerned about prior.

Due to Google Sheets’ limitation that every user must have the same experience for updated information, some information wasn’t essential to both user groups, or didn’t matter after a certain point, yet was kept for historical purposes. For example, CaseStack users minded the status and times more prior to Check In, while the Warehouse users only monitored timing after Check In. Extraneous data for different parties made it difficult to focus on the important information, to understand the situation, and to know next actions.

Example of CaseStack View — what we researched and simplified

Design Goal: Simplify information overload for specific contexts & users

Why did they need this? Historical reasons? Or I don’t care about it after this point? We researched all the “whys” for every data point’s value and usage.

Visuals with recognizable icons and reduced importance to secondary items help reduce information overload, yet maintains clarity. For example, we used retailer icons that were easily recognizable.

Designing specific views for individual parties’ goals and hiding unnecessary information in the right context helps add focus. For example, the ETA no longer mattered when the carrier was already checked in. Warehouse users valued Appointment time more for prioritizing work than CaseStack users.

CaseStack View (left) and Warehouse View (right)

Unable to Follow Context

A commenting experience that utilizes visual consistency, removes visual clutter with resolved issues, and maintains history.

How can we solve the pain points of 1) comments taking up too much space, and 2) unable to follow the conversation easily until you look closely at the times? Also note how the the cells grow as the comment gets longer

The system for comments is that a single column was assigned to a group, so each would archive previous comments and reuse the same cell. This method helped easily identify the party and who to contact. However, there was no chronological order for the comments, even though the most recent comment is most valued, regardless of who wrote it.

Due to Google Sheets’ limitations, following the chronological conversation to know next steps was difficult, and comments were often deleted.

Old comments were deleted to indicate “no issues to address”, but historical data was lost. Some people accidentally wrote over comments so context was lost. Cells grew as the comments got longer, making it hard to scan each vertical line.

Design Goal: Make Comments more understandable & usable without creating issues

Based on the research, comments needed to include:

  • show commented user group
  • order comments in chronological order, regardless of user
  • make issues resolved disappear visually, but keep for history for easy issue tracking

A seamless commenting experience that includes features that follow their mental model, show only what’s needed at the moment, and keep visual consistency reduces confusion & provides better understanding. Ordering the comments temporally, while clearly indicating the party, make the comments much easier to follow. We learned the presence of comments indicates issues were currently happening with that load. Allowing them to “Resolve Issue” by archiving the current comments indicates “no issues” visually on that load while keeping historical data. To reduce visual clutter and keep visual consistency of each load vertically, we only display what’s needed, meaning the most recent and initial comment, until its looked into more.

Show Lo-fi Sooner, and Balance!

Unfortunately, I left Casestack prior to learning if the new product helped with improving on-time metrics or if less loads were pushed out to the next day. I hope a better design helped react to issues quicker! For all products I have designed, I iterated the most for this design. Usability testing kept coming back unsuccessful, especially for visualizing the statuses — we continued relentlessly at new ideas and sessions. I was reminded to not focus on the detailed pixels for each new iteration, but rather showing lo-fi designs to users sooner rather than later. Another takeaway was finding the balance between reducing clutter and presenting visual clarity. Icons without labels didn’t test very well, considering the current Excel and the types of users. Thank you design team and Alpaca Engineering team for all your feedback, time, and hard work! Nothing is ever accomplished alone :)
