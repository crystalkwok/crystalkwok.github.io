Retain Enterprise Customers

Bluebeam’s product team recently shifted focus onto better retaining enterprise customers, characterized by:

  • working on bigger projects with more external (outside the company) collaborators
  • more internal employees with high turnover
  • heavy emphasis on security & legal requirements

Characterized by more employees, focusing on enterprise customers would give us more revenue based on our pricing structure at the time. On top of that, our competitors often geared towards enterprise customers, and we wanted to ensure we didn’t lose customers to our competitors in that market.

Low-Hanging Fruit in “Managing People in Spaces”

As a new team, we wanted to start afresh. We conducted qualitative research with about 7 enterprise sized customers, asking them about how they used Studio, what were the most important pain points, the reason behind their pain points or potential solutions, and more. Most common problems lied in:

  • editing other people’s markups / notes (for various reasons that could be solved in different ways)
  • updating new plans (they are different in different stages)
  • creating new spaces
  • & more!

We dug into why users mentioned pain points or feature requests, to see if we could solve in various ways with given constraints. Together with engineering, we knew that for some of the problems, the right way would require massive technical rework, such as a new structure of Studio. We decided as a team to solve problems within “creating a new space,” which did not demand a completely new structure, and would provide value to our customers while we planned out how to provide significant value with technical rework.

**As reflection on how we could improve, I wish we had done more market research on enterprise customers to better choose and prioritize what work to work on for the most impact.

Biggest Pain Point: Inviting People for Enterprise

We also conducted multiple research studies with contextual inquiry, scripted interviews, and usability tests with 7 additional customers and internal industry experts, with multiple iterations and explorations of designs. We researched with enterprise and SMB customers, to make sure we didn’t isolate other customers with new designs.

Wireframes and Flows

Through our research, we found that the biggest pain point with creating new spaces was the process of inviting people, especially for enterprise customers, because of more people to invite, higher turnover, and higher security requirements.

You can only input one email at a time. How sadly inefficient

We found that typically, people did not use the in-app method to invite people. However, outside methods didn’t allow the collaboration space to be locked to only those invited, so whoever had the ID could join. In order to block someone’s access, they would have had to join before the Studio space could block their access, or even give them the right permission. What terrible security!

We heard 5/7 of our participants forgo security by using outside-app methods to invite for ease of use, even if they value security. Most copied a link to the invitation and sent it via their work email, because moderators could:

  • easily access the emails of internal and external folks right from Outlook with autocomplete
  • invite many, or even the same, people with ease (one had an email template), since Enterprise projects had a lot of internal and external collaborators
  • see who had actually been invited, and compare & contrast who had joined or not joined yet
  • write an individualized message with a due date & email signature

2/7 were legally required to use more secure access (i.e. sensitive information) and used the in-app method. One complained about how they took 30 minutes every time to invite people, and another used a hack by creating fake “Contact Groups” in Outlook. People would often pawn it off to associates as “menial work” or have dedicated people just for inviting & managing people.

4/7 people would list having to invite everyone again as a reason why they wouldn’t create new collaboration spaces, and would therefore ask for other features to avoid creating new spaces.

Using the hack Contact Groups from Outlook to add a lot of people

Increase In-app Inviting with Security & Participation

After our initial research, our product team decided to focus on:

  • improving the in-app inviting experience of Studio to increase stickiness of the product & reduce risk (ask me for more info!)
  • stressing security so people wouldn’t have to choose between secure access or ease of adding others
  • keeping legal requirements and project deadlines by ensuring particular parties are participating & reviewing so the project can move forward, as more emphasized by Enterprise companies.
How can we make our in-app invite process better to easily maintain security, keep legal requirements, and meet deadlines?

We decided our success metrics would be to:

  • increase the number of people using the Studio invite process
  • increase the percentage of secured spaces
  • improve participation rates as legally required, and meet deadlines

Too Long to Invite Many People in Multiple Parties

A flexible way to invite many people from multiple sources shortens the invite time and allows for easy secure access. Email domain organization helps ensure all necessary parties are invited to prevent legal issues.

During our research, each collaboration space typically has a “moderator”, who manages the people aspect, such as ensuring “participants” join. The previous in-app method only allowed you to put in one email at a time. Our Outlook integration only showed people that were within the company, so inviting external people was still individually typed. Even though security was a concern, moderators utilized workarounds & outside methods to invite many people with ease & fulfill other needs, but had no secure access because participants would have to join before any action could be taken, like blocking or giving the right permission.

People copy the invitation and send it out via an external email

Design Goal: Make inviting many people easier with contact information from multiple sources

We found that teams stored the list of contact information to invite in easily accessible places for the team, but in different formats. For example, one was an email forwarded to them, saying “invite everyone on this email chain”; another was an Excel sheet on their network drive; another was on Google Docs. It could also be from multiple sources at a time because different companies send you their own list of people to invite. Remember, enterprise customers were more likely to work with more external collaborators.

A flexible solution that allows many people to be invited from any source(s) in-app helps with easily accessing contact information, saving time, and allowing for more secure access. Moderators can copy and paste many emails from anywhere (Outlook, Excel, etc.), and we’ll only take the emails, and ignore special characters and names. People won’t have to clean up the email list, regardless of what the source includes. No user asked for this, but this understood the user’s context and it became a highlighted delighter. To accommodate similar people always working together and being invited, we also made our Groups feature more discoverable that many didn’t know about. This makes it easier to handle the constant churn of new internal people coming into a project that often happens with Enterprise companies.

Ideating (and then usability tested) on 2 different approaches to utilizing multiple methods to add people. #2 One at a time approach was the final chosen flow!

Organizing the emails by domain increases accuracy of inviting different parties to prevent legal issues & deadline delays. Since there were often multiple sources / lists from multiple external collaborators, moderators who often invited external people loved the domain separation because domains are an indicator of companies, so they could double-check easily if all the people that need to be involved were invited. For example, they’ll need to invite 4 subcontractors, but they’ll forget to include the electrical team since they sent over a separate list. Particular parties/roles must review the contracts or drawings before moving forward legally.

Invites Missed & Who’s Not Here Yet?

Moderators can see & remind people who haven’t joined. Participants more easily access invites in-app, so participation increases as legally required and projects don’t delay, incurring more cost.

We found moderators also liked sending invites through their own email for:

  • visibility on who they invited before to compare and remind those who hadn’t joined to participate by the deadline

Studio previously only let you see who had joined, but not who hadn’t.

We also found that participants don’t join as fast because:

  • Their invites get lost amongst their email clutter
  • Emails from Studio land into the “Spam” folder and are missed

It is imperative that participants join (ideally sooner) because particular roles are legally required to review the plans in the space. For example, you cannot build a building without having structural engineers review and approve architects’ work, which typically requires collaborative conversations. However, if they are unaware of where to review work, the deadlines will be missed and projects will be delayed, costing more money, since they cannot move forward until reviewed. Participation, which is legally required, is slower or even missed due to lack of visibility for moderators and invites being missed for participants, which pushes back deadlines.

Design Goal: Get more people to join sooner so they participate on time

We asked our moderators what they typically did after they saw who hadn’t joined yet, and 7/7 responded they would typically remind them to join and what the deadline was. We also found that participants find knowing who hadn’t joined yet helpful because they’ll know that someone hadn’t responded yet because they hadn’t joined. Visibility to who hasn’t joined & allowing moderators to remind them helps easily manage people in-app to ensure meeting legal requirements.

Participants can now more easily join in-app, which our loyal customers are typically on all day. While we can’t stop emails from going into Spam, we can help them easily join. Making it easier for participants to join increases participation as legally required and prevents project delays & increasing cost.

Future Initiative to Improve Ease of Use for Security

This project isn’t full of new, innovative interactions and can almost seem quite obvious, especially at first. However, it was a practice in identifying valuable ways to improve experiences that solves pain points and meet business goals that users won’t mention because they are so familiar with their current process.

Future initiatives with this team continued to improve ease of use for security, especially for Enterprise customers. One of the reasons why people wanted to edit other’s markups / notes was because people would leave the company (higher company turnover within Enterprise companies), but no one else could edit their work. This would be detrimental if they wrote something incorrect that was shown to other external teams, or clients & owners of the projects. To provide value within our technical constraint (only one person can edit their assigned markup), we allowed moderators to deny people and reassign their markups / notes to one other person already on the Studio space to edit and manage. Studio invites was the base that makes the security & management aspect possible, so the people management aspect can start right away without having to wait for someone to join, so only the right people can have the right access.

Other future initiatives working on applying the right permissions easily across different companies and roles, so security can be maintained even from the start without waiting for someone to join before giving them the right permissions or restrictions.
