Ready to Install

Increasing Ease of Use & Likely to Use > 30% for new product by connecting materials’ progress to installation tasks to know if ready to install

Learn User Goal of Managing Task Information

When I first joined the team, the team had already conducted some research, and decided to focus on managing installation tasks, and material deliveries, issues, and documents within it. We knew that there were no competitors that had great usability for managing materials (ex: shipping steel bars for a structural install on a construction site) for Subcontractors in the field, and based on how people had been managing it, the pain point was that all information was so separate.

But what was the point of managing all this? Management in itself is not a pain point. The lack of purpose for the product was evident, because no customer had understood and were willing to purchase the product.

Learn User Goal of Managing Task Information

Previous research showed that current tools used to see task schedules (typically Gantt charts) cannot hold more construction specific details for each task outside of the location, name, and dates, supposedly differentiating our product from competitors like Microsoft Projects and Procore, although most used Excel. I originally was tasked with redesigning details in an installation task, but the initiative lacked a goal. I organized 6 research sessions to better understand what construction people wanted to understand from an installation task, so I could learn how we could differentiate from competitors and prioritize what information to put first.

1. Microsoft Projects 2. Task Details to redesign-- but what's the goal?

I asked questions such as:

  • How do you currently collaborate regarding any details about a task past the name and location? What works and doesn’t?
  • When are some times when you’ve looked into understanding more details around a task? (to understand context)
  • What information are you looking for to understand the task in more detail?

I found that:

  • 4/6 participants wanted to see more task details generally during planning meetings in order to know what constraints/blockers they had before moving forward.
  • 4/6 participants mentioned constraints or blockers as the most important information to know about a task, which included:
    • Materials
    • Issues
    • Documents Approved (or not)
    • Predecessor Dependent Tasks

The research details

While Issues and Docs can also typically change in the middle of installation, all of the items were mostly for Pre-Installation. Essentially, you could not start the installation task itself until you had cleared these blockers or understood the information provided. It seemed that the necessity of knowing the status of the materials, issues, and documents, in the end, was to know what installation tasks were ready to install in order to get to the next step (Installation). Because all the information was kept so separately to manage, there was no way to see how it affected the installation task it associated with.

I also had more confirmations to my hypothesis as one of our potential customers mentioned as a few of the pain points:

  • They always checked 7 different Google sheets every morning, each with a different kind of blocker (an Issues dashboard, Deliveries coming today, the Fabrication Shop status on Materials, etc.) to check what the blockers associated were still open before they could working on it.
  • When knowing what materials will come in a delivery on this date, they wanted to know what installation task it referred to so they could know whether it would come by the scheduled start date and if they could start working on it right away. This is also so they match the right material to the right install so they can install it right to avoid rework if it was installed incorrectly.

2/7 Google sheets they check every morning-- has the same trusses on there, but show different information

A sample email showing the materials arriving today for research

Product Hypothesis: People in the field want to know-- how can clear these blockers so we get this installation task Ready to Install by this date?

We also found that 5/5 participants said it was confusing to understand what happened in our task because there were not Material line items. Our structure was not sufficient-- it included Deliveries, but did not include the specific Materials that were needed for the installations.

Focus on Materials for Pre-Install

Melody, our customer success manager took a poll to a construction focused user base (yes, on Instagram), and we found that any problems re: materials had the least visibility and was most desired to know right away of all the constraints. On top of that, materials has the most complex process to manage with various parties (Procurement, Shop, Field Team, Project Managers) compared to issues and documents. There was also no great existing competitor management system for materials with installation tasks, while many have tackled the issues / document space.

Yes I know...Instagram surveys probably don't produce the best survey results, especially with the small sample size we had, and I would probably reword the question again if I could. But I think it certainly provides a good indicator on where to start looking!

From research above, I created these to show & align the team how we could achieve this purpose in the product, and what we learned in the field.

As a first step, we also updated the details in the Task to at least make all the necessary pre-steps and steps scannable, so field teams could easily see what was done or still open. The previous design had Subtasks and Materials hidden in various tabs and sections.

Logistics Problems - Not all Materials Ready, Need Partial Builds

Materials don’t come in the right order or time, but the field needs to keep working. Ideally, all materials would come in the order and right before the start date according to the installation schedule. However, vendors won’t have certain parts available, and lead times (time it takes to make the material) are often not accurate even if ordered on time, so many end up saying “just send me whatever you have,” so they can start on some of the work, even not in the right order, or they’ll pay laborers for not working, or their laborers will leave for other jobs. Therefore, many installation tasks will have some materials ready, but not all. But how do you know which work has its materials ready so it can start, or can’t start and what materials are they waiting on?

1. What they're using now to see what has all its materials arriving.
2. Not all materials have arrived, so they use wood during partial builds to substitute the missing part

Only a limited amount of materials should be delivered and stored. You may also ask, “Why not have all your materials ready way before installation starts?” Unfortunately, working and storage space is valuable & limited in a shop or onsite, so you can’t have all the materials for the whole project at a time. Also, materials are more likely to be damaged or stolen as you store them longer. Therefore, people have typically done “just in time” deliveries, where only a limited amount of materials are delivered for the tasks planned as needed. This eliminates waste, according to lean construction, although it seems like it creates the problem of materials more likely to be missed.

Material Progress often Separated from Installation because Different User Needs

The shop/vendor managing materials and sending updates on progress is different than how the field needs to see it with installation. Why has it been difficult to see what installation tasks have all their materials delivered? Typically, a material’s progress and delivery is managed and communicated by the vendor or a prefabrication shop (creates pre-made parts or sections). They simply care about getting the materials requested done and shipped out, and not what installation it ties into on the construction site. Most have one area for the vendors or shop to manage materials, such as a single sheet in Google Sheets, Vista, or Stratus, organized by materials they need to get done.

What the shop / vendors use usually to manage materials & deliveries without installation tasks (Stratus, Vista)

The field team needs to know the status of materials to plan out their work, but they mostly focus on their installation task. If a material is delivering today, what work can the field start working on today? Also, if the field are hoping to start this work by next week, how many materials are they still waiting on, and will it deliver by the time it should start?

Currently, the shop / vendor and the field have multiple sheets managing different types of blockers, but also have the same blockers organized differently to based on how other parties prefer it. For example, one might be organized by what order contains what material, another has what deliveries for the materials are coming today, while another is how the shop prefers to see their work on materials to do. This makes every party have to update multiple places with the same information. One of our users says she updates at least 4 different sheets simply if a delivery comes to communicate with everyone, let alone all other kinds of updates to communicate. Therefore, tracking down all the materials needed and its progress for the upcoming work requires checking multiple sheets & jumping around everywhere.

1. The field teams planning out work and seeing which ones still have blockers open. When asked if they would include materials on there, they would (2)!

3. Just the sheet to tracking what deliveries are coming. What about all those other sheets we've seen earlier that they need to mark?

Focused Spaces for Different User Needs, but Connected Information

** I used the existing design system to design these new experiences, but I'll be showing another design with a different design system later.

A focused space where a shop / vendor can easily see their work to be done, and quickly manage the status of each material helps them communicate effectively and timely without updating in multiple places. This to-do list won't be muddled with other items on the level, like tasks.

Connecting the information updated by the shop/vendor to where the field see their tasks to be done with the materials’ information meets the field team’s needs to know how blockers like the materials affect the start of an installation. The field team won’t have to hunt through different sheets of various blockers to understand information about one particular task. A shop / vendor can also easily note any problems they are trying to fix for a material, and the information will be passed along when evaluating when to start installation so the field team can easily know what is or isn’t being blocked to start working.

Showing the data in a smart way visualizes and notifies the field team of work that could be worked on right away, without having holdup periods and delays for the team, and have laborers be paid without working. If all materials have arrived, they can know that this task, or even a small subtask can be done now (if it did not depend on other work to be done first). If materials have been scheduled to be delivered, they can know what will be ready soon, and the date to start the installation based on the delivery dates.

If they want to start an installation task soon but it’s still not ready, they can easily see its blockers by knowing what materials are still needed, its progress based on what the vendor/shop say, what could be the holdup based on its issues, and follow up with those particular vendor/shop if need be.

38% Increase for Likely to Use, 32% Increase for Ease of Use

Previous Research - Current Product Ratings

In our previous research, I asked the questions at the end:

  • Rate on a scale of 1-4 the use of use in finding the information you are looking for to understand and figure out next steps for this task.
  • On a scale of 1-10, how likely are you to start using this to collaborate on deliveries and subtasks over your current methods?

Ease of Use - Avg 2.9/4

Most mentioned it was not enough to only see deliveries, but they wanted the individual materials inside. They couldn’t see how the individual materials connected directly to which individual installation subtasks (linking materials directly to what is to be installed!) They also mentioned that deliveries could hold materials for more than just one task.

Likely to Use - Avg 5.6/10

Most asked “is this just double work for me?” and didn’t see the purpose of needing to manage their work here and somewhere else.

** I definitely learned from this that I probably should have just used the same scale for both.

New Concept Research

I created a new concept with the assets above to research with construction people how valuable this might be. With the previously shown experience, you could easily see how materials being delivered would immediately show which particular part of an installation task was ready to install (while others were not), while the shop in charge of materials could manage it without the task. I also showed how materials, not deliveries, tied back to the installation.

To try to keep the results consistent, I only mostly changed aspects of the design that related to the specific functionality that I wanted to test (new structure, overview information).

Goals

My goal was to understand if there was a significant demand to know what's ready to be installed, and how it compared relative to our current product. With this problem, the user group that would gain the most value and would want to know this information would be the field team, since there currently are tools for the shop / vendors (Stratus, Vista, etc.), and asking them to switch tools would be a bigger barrier, so we'd likely do an integration with those tools. Would field people involved with planning and installing find it valuable to know which tasks had all their materials delivered so they could start their work?

1. Showing overviews (Not Ready, 2/4 Materials Not Delivered) on installation schedule
2. Cleaned up list to see Installation Tasks & the hierarchy with Materials.
3. Connecting materials to the right subtasks, and can see which subtasks are ready with all their materials.

I also wanted to know if the new structure better matched their mental model with deliveries and its individual line items with materials. The new structure should make it easy to see what's delivery today and mark it delivered, while maintaining the line item materials connection to tasks. The change from the old product includes changing the information architecture & navigation names to not be based its design, but based on its content (Materials and Deliveries, not List and Schedule).

1. Change in structure & navigation for the new prototype.
2. With the new structure & navigation, hopefully it'll be easier to view what's coming in what delivery and mark them delivered. Imagine trying to filter down to just materials and not know what will be in the delivery, and marking them all individually as delivered.

Methodology

I created a prototype with the new concept with “realistic data” (as best Melody in customer success and I could), and I inserted the same data into the current product. I asked our participants to complete 2 scenarios that they typically did on a daily or weekly basis. Our scenarios were:

  • You are planning out work for this upcoming 6 weeks. Determine what your next steps would be from the information.
  • You were told in an email that this delivery with 2 materials would actually be coming today. Mark this delivery as delivered.

I conducted the research study with 6 participants (people involved with planning out the work onsite), where each completed the same tasks in both. I had half of the participants start with the current product, and the other half start with the new prototype. At the end, I asked them to rate both with 2 questions:

  • Rate on a scale of 1-10 the ease of use in finding the information you are looking for to understand and figure out next steps, with 1 being the least easy to use, and 10 being the most easy to use.
  • On a scale of 1-10 for both the prototype and the website, how likely are you to start using this to collaborate on tasks, materials, and deliveries?

Results

  Ease of Use Likely to Use
Current Product Avg 5.6 Avg 5.2
New Prototype Avg 7.4 Avg 7.2
% Change 32% 38%

This increase, while not hugely significant, is a step in the right direction. In the previous test, the Likely to Use score was also around 5 (Avg 5.6), so likely a bigger sample size would result in a similar rating for the old product.

While it might not have the be the perfect solution, they certainly found more value in the new concept. Some of the feedback were:

  • While the automated statuses “Not Ready” or “Ready to Install” didn’t matter to them, the information that fed into that status was valuable to them as an overview. Likely, we would not use these exact statuses and simply tell them the information -- “1 Delivered” or “2/3 Materials Not Delivered.” I would be curious how it might change if we introduced other Pre-Install items, like Approved Documents.
  • Seeing the content as the name as opposed to “List” for the Deliveries and Materials was more clear in finding what they need.
  • People mostly stayed within the schedule-- that might be their preference to stay within one place, without jumping around to see the information. Users also felt like other modals made them lost if it brought them to another modal, and couldn’t click back to see the previous information.
    I would also research if they mostly stayed on the schedule because that's the typical way how they know what tasks will occur (Gantt charts from Microsoft Projects), or if they would benefit from another design, like a list so they can see immediately what needs to be done right after another.

More Testing, Research with Procurement Team

I proposed our next steps were to continue researching if it was still valuable to field teams by iterating on the prototype and testing more, and having the Product Managers start market research to be more confident if this product would sell.

If the end product vision is still considered valuable to field teams, I would recommend our next steps be to research with teams who procure what materials are needed for a project, which could be a particular procurement manager or a project engineer. These people are key in the beginning for the end vision to work-- tying all materials needed to the installation task in the beginning before the work starts. The concern is that it would be too manual and the team would not be motivated to do this detailed work that they don’t do currently. I would research ways we could shorten or at least keep the same amount of time they spend currently writing out different materials needed with a new process with the tasks. Some ideas to shorten the amount of time would be material templates for particular tasks (ex: all piping installs needs these 3 materials).

I also wanted create a design with a different design system, and more optimized for mobile and tablet. I expect some aspects would be used in mobile, such as accessing information in the field as you see an issue, or on the tablet, when they do morning huddles to talk about what they will be working on that day. Both instances will need the information on materials and tasks.
