LEARNING EQUALITY

designing the content curation experience for teachers providing offline education to third world countries

Learning Equality is a non-profit that seeks to enable universal access to high quality open educational resources, and to create tools that empower communities to author and share their own content. Learning Equality is committed to enabling every person in the world to achieve a quality basic education. I interned here as a user experience design intern, and was part of process of designing the experience and interface of the content curation process.

Content Curation: First Iteration Research & Design

the challenge

Our team had been tasked with designing the content curation experience, where teachers can add and share their own content to teach their students on this platform. This was based on a simple idea, with very little direction (which is a good and bad thing!) to what we should start designing. This platform had to allow for small amounts of content, as well as as much content as Khan Academy has in a single account. Finding a way to organize and display massive amounts of data is definitely a challenge.

our approach and process


We conducted user research on Noemi from War Child Holland, who has no interface to curate her own content directly. Through our user research, we also found the distinction between :


  • content driven curation: the user usually dumps all their content into the platform, then organizes it gradually through the whole process, often changing where it goes
  • topic driven curation: the user already has in mind the types of topics they want, and exactly where the content would go. The user usually organizes and creates the topics before they put in the content.

Noemi mentioned that she was often unsure of how everything would fit together and would move her content around a lot. So based on user research, we decided to make the platform also catered to topic-driven curation style, which is why we created the workspace where users can dump their content in first.

Based on our research, we decided to create wireframes, continue with design critique sessions, and create hi-fidelity prototypes.

Content Curation: Qualitative User Research pt.2

the challenge

After creating our prototypes, we wanted to see if people understood what we had designed, and if it made sense in acheiving the goals of a content curator. We also wanted to see if it fit the mental models of content curators, or how people organized related things together.

our approach and process

Parallel Research

The user starts with a bottom up method, by grabbing all the related content items related to one single item and grouping them together

The user ends up with a vertical hierarchy when she is done organizing things together, and clumps together everything that is related

We conducted some usability tests with the talk-out-loud method on our current prototypes with our fellow interns, since access to content curators is often difficult. We watched for their pain points and how they interacted with our prototypes.

We also used the parallel research method to get a better idea of the mental model people have when organizing related objects. We did this by giving users slips of paper with different kinds of content (ex: Introduction to Math, Calculus I, etc.) to organize and group together, in order to watch how people usually organize content together. This gave us an idea into the mental model a user has in doing an activity, and what they would naturally do and how they would think during the activity.


Here are some of our insights:

talk-out-loud method:

  • showing the end product always helps them with knowing how they should start building it
  • the wording of "topic" and "content" are unfamiliar to users-- possibly renaming to "folder" and "files"
  • a blank screen in the beginning is never great-- every user needs at least a first step guidance, or should include inline-help
  • take out redundancy-- it'll confuse the users

parallel research method:

  • users first separated by the overall idea first
  • users first thought of the general idea, took out the content, then separated them into even more smaller sections
  • users grouped content together first and then created topic names
  • users don't like a lot of levels-- only if there is a massive amount of content will they create more levels to organize
  • users usually created a vertical hierarchy to visualize their content

the outcome

From our user research, we concluded the following design changes should be made:

  • first step guidance
  • renaming to fit the mental models in the users' heads
  • creating a workspace where people can dump their content (upload or drag and drop) their first in there, then drag their finalized groupings into the finalized topic tree. This is to fit the bottom-up process users usually use
