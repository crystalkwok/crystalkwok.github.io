MYEPLAY


DURATION:   September 2014 - December 2014

ROLE:   UX / UI / Interaction Designer

TOOLS:   Adobe Creative Suite, Wireframing, Prototyping, User Stories/ Scenarios, NodeJS, HTML/CSS

DESCRIPTION:   eye tracking based entertainment suite

COLLABORATORS:   Alex Yang, Andrew Wang, Danny Zhang, Kane Chong, Luke Kim, Sam Suen, Sean Burke, Pavan Kumar

DELIVERABLES:  Website   |    GitHub    |    User Story    |    Final Presentation



Meet Bob Veillette: a man who has Locked-in Syndrome that leaves him paralyzed except for his eyes. Our team had been tasked with creating an application geared towards with LIS, and more specifically, Bob. Loved ones usually only have one-way communication with people with LIS. LIS patients have also communicated that they feel trapped in a limiting body, which they can still hear and be cognizant of the world around them, and their ability to think has not been damaged. Also, people with LIS often find themseves in boredom because of their lack of ability to do a variety of activities. In our eye-tracking based entertainment suite, we have included games, which can be played as a single or two player, and a media player that utilizes the YouTube API. Our main goals for MyePlay is for Bob to be able to relieve his boredom, socially interact with his loved ones, and enlighten his mind through videos.

DESIGNING FOR THE EYE & LIS
[ more info ]
When we were designing the experience, interaction, and interface, we were faced with an interesting problem. Bob's, as our main user, eyes are mainly restricted to vertical movement, and although a few eye-tracking applications have been made for people with LIS, Bob's involuntary flutter in his left eye has left him unable to use these already existing applications. Aside from Bob, people normally do not have to use their eyes use their eyes for more than simply noticing and looking at something, which means this application would be specially geared towards not only Bob, but those who actually do not have their full bodily capabilities. These users often truly rely on technology to assist them in performing normal operations, as opposed to those who do have fully functioning bodies who most only use technology to enhance their lives. Having the chance to design for people with bodily limitations is important because designers usually only design for people whom society deems "normal", but has neglected those who cannot fit that standard. Designers have often not chosen to focus on these users because they do not have full capabilities, and it often limits what kind of interactions can be designed into the system. What is intuitive and easy to use is different for each of the different kinds of users. However, we do not see these users as limitations, but rather new possibilities for a new user interface, new user experience, and interactions. This opens up new possibilities to rethink already existing functions and purposes of the different parts of the users' bodies, because people with LIS must learn to use their eyes for more functions and purposes than people with full capabilies usually need to. Although studies have shown that using the eye for more than attention creates more problems and errors, we believe that MyePlay has been able to redefine the eye's purpose and function. The eye is usually seen as a passive way to indicate visual attention, but MyePlay creates the new possibility of using the eye as an active decision maker.

FIRST PROTOTYPE

Being the only designer on the team, I was in charge of designing the user experience, user interface, and interaction of the entire entertainment suite, although I mostly worked on the menu and how to move through it and choose different options. As a designer, I had to take into account not only Bob's limitations for only using his eyes, but also the hardware limitations of a cheaper eyetracker. After the software engineers gave me a list of eye movements they could possibly use as events in Javascript, I designed off of these eye movements for our first prototype using Adobe Illustrator, and I created a demo video that highlights the UI and interactions to show our investors at the Moxie Center. In general, my goal was to make the user interface as intuitive as possible, and the interactions as simple as possible, to keep the number of errors our user could make to a minimum.

FINAL WORKING PROTOTYPE
[ more info ]
After extensive testing with the EyeTribe (the eye tracker we were using), we decided to only have a vertical spiral design with no flat button design, because it would only show one option at once, preventing errors that could easily be made by the system with flat button design. Although MyePlay ended up being simpler than we originally imagined due to hardware limitations and time constraints, we still believe MyePlay has fulfilled its goals of relieving boredom, fostering social interaction, and enlightening the user with knowledge to improve the user's quality of life. We feel privileged to have been able to design something to improve and enrich Bob's life, and to have redefined the eye's purpose and function as not just a passive way to indicate attention, but an active decision maker. This demo is of our final design and project.
