FlunkLess     top design in hci course

DURATION: April 2014- June 2014

ROLE: UX/UI Designer

COLLABORATORS:
Vlad Bakhurinsky, Michelle Wang, Jared Defigh, Meena Kaushik, Hui Ping Lee, Lucas DePaula, Alex Peterson, Crystal Kwok

METHODS:
Contextual Inquiry, Ethnographic Studies, Affinity Diagramming, Persona Writing, Cultural and Sequence Modelling, Visioning, Wireframing, Prototyping, Competitive Analysis

DESCRIPTION: synchronous communication between professors, teaching assisants, and students during lecture

Final Executive Summary   |   Final Presentation Slides   |   Final Paper

what's the PROBLEM?

The motivation behind Flunkless is this question: how do you effectively engage and motivate participation from over a hundred students in a college lecture? In large universities, such as UCSD, the current way lectures work is that the communication is only from the professor to the students, but not the other way aroud. Also, many students are losing interest during lecture and going to their laptops for distractions. We decided to address this issue by creating a virtual space to ask questions, post comments, and share links DURING LECTURE to enable active participation of multiple students at the same time.

how did you get there?

methods used:
contextual inquiry
affinity diagram

Our original idea was a way to integrate academics and Facebook together. We decided to conduct interviews of students, teaching assistants, and professors based on how they use Facebook or other tools (like Piazza and Blackboard) for academics.

After 12 interviews, we consolidated our user data into an affinity diagram, which is a way to visualize the patterns of the different issues and insights presented by our users that easily reveals our users' needs.

what did you learn from your data?

methods used:
persona writing
cultural and sequence modelling
visioning
wireframing
prototyping

Some main insights we gained through our data was:
- students wanted to share their ideas, work, and other resources
- students wanted a way to confidently ask questions instead of being intimidated by the staff
- students wanted to be able to meet other students in the class
- TAs did not want to mix their social and professional lives through Facebook
- professors wanted their students to be more engaged with the material
- professors and TAs did not want to answer the same question over and over again
- all users wanted easy communication with each other


Through this data, we were able to create three personas for the professor, TA, and student. We also created two sequence models and a cultural model.

We also started visioning for our users, as well as wireframing and paper prototyping for future interviews. We decided to go for a synchronous communication between professors, TAs, and students in place of office hours.

what next?

methods used:
contextual inquiry
affinity diagram

We decided to continue with user research and conducted 6 more interviews with our new wireframes and paper prototypes to test if it fit the users' needs and passed usability tests. However, users mentioned that our ideas were nothing new and did not like the ideas. However, when we posed the question if we were to offer synchronous communication during lecture, users seemed to really like this new idea. Originally, we thought the problem was finding a more effective way for nonsynchronous communication, but user data has shown us that the user need is for synchronous communication during lecture, which is impossible in a large lecture without technology. This is reflective of a paper written by a prominent HCI professor, Professor Jim Hollan, who mentions that technology's purpose shound not be to replicate face-to-face interaction, but to go beyond being there and to meet needs that are not met even with physical proximity.



what are your features?

methods used:
competitive analysis

For our synchronous communication during lecture for effective communication between a large number of students and the teachers during lecture, we decided to use these features to meet user needs from our interviews:

1. ASK AWAY-- post your questions as they come to mind.
Students wanted to ask questions right away to avoid forgetting their question, asking an intimidating professor, and waiting in line to talk to the professor.

2. SHARE IT-- share related links you stumble upon, or pictures of your notes
Students like to share material to help others out.

3. VOTE-- popular questions go to the top, creating priority ranking
TAs and professors often find repeating questions, and TAs have to try to find the important questions and issues rather than it being readily available.

4. CHECKPOINTS-- for professors to gather instant feedback on students' progress
Professors want to know if they have been misunderstood or confusing, and to know if the students are understanding the material.


We have found that no current websites, applications, or tools fulfill this need. Piazza offers nonsynchronous communication Q&A platform, Facebook does not support effective and easy communication between our users, the iClicker only has options A-D for questions the professor offers (but not the students), and Blackboard's (TED's) usability is extremely poor and does not offer synchronous communication.
